序

Data are all around us and always have been. Everything throughout history has always had the potential to be quantified: theoretically，one could count every hu¬man who has ever lived，every heartbeat that has ever beaten，every step that was ever taken，every star that has ever shone，every word that has ever been uttered or written. Each of these collective things can be represented by a number. But on¬ly recently have we had the technology to efficiently surface these hidden num¬bers， leading to greater insight into our human condition.

数据都在我们身边，一直都是。在历史上一直都很有潜力：从理论上讲，人们可以指望哪里曾经住每个人，每一次心跳，什么时候采取的哪一个步骤，哪里照耀每一个明星，哪个字曾经被说出或写出。这些集体的东西可以用数量来表示。但直到最近，我们有技术，能够有效地浮现这些隐藏的数字，从而更深入地了解我们的过去。

But what does this mean, exactly? What are the cultural effects of having easy access to data? It means, for one thing, that we all need to be more data literate. It also means we have to be more design literate. As the old adage goes, statistics lie. Well, data visualizations lie, too. How can we learn how to first, effectively read data visualizations; and second, author them in such a way that is ethical and clearly communicates the data’s inherent story?

但是，这代表什么？轻松访问数据会对文化有什么影响？这意味着，对于一件事，我们都需要更多的数据读写。这也意味着我们需要提高设计素养。正如老话所说，统计撒谎。那么，被数据可视化骗了。我们怎样才能学会？第一，有效地读取数据的可视化；第二，作者他们以这样的方式明确传达了数据中的故事吗？

At the intersection of art and algorithm, data visualization schematically abstracts information to bring about a deeper understanding of the data, wrapping it in an element of awe.

Maria Popova, Stories for the Information Age, Businessweek

在艺术和算法，数据可视化示意图的摘要信息的交汇点，以实现对数据的更深入的了解，封装在敬畏中的一个元素。

玛丽亚·波波娃，信息时代的故事，商业周刊

My favorite description of data visualization comes from the prolific blogger, Maria Popova, who said that data visualization is “at the intersection of art and algorithm.” To learn about the history of data visualization is to become an armchair cartographer, explorer, and statistician.

我最喜欢的数据可视化的描述来自于多产的博客，玛丽亚·波波娃，谁表示，“数据可视化“，在艺术和算法的交叉点。”要了解数据可视化的历史，就是成为一个扶手椅制图员，探险家和统计学家。

Early visual explorations of data focused mostly on small snippets of data gleaned to expand humanity’s understanding of the geographical world, mainly through maps. Starting with the first recognized world maps of the 13th century, scientists, mathematicians, philosophers, and sailors used math to visualize the invisible. Stars and suns were plotted, coastlines and shipping routes charted. Data visualization, in its native essence, drew the lines, points, and coordinates that gave form to the physical world and our place in it. It answered questions like “Where am I?”, “How do I get there?”, and “How far is it?”

大多集中在小片段数据的可视化早期探索收集，扩大地理世界人类的了解，主要是通过地图。在13世纪的第一个公认的世界地图开始，科学家，数学家，哲学家，和水手使用的数学可视化无形。星星和太阳绘制，海岸线和航线绘制。数据可视化，在其原生的本质，提请线，点和坐标的形式给到物理世界，我们在其中的位置。它回答的问题，比如“我在哪里？”，“我如何到达那里？”和“有多远？”

Early data visualizations were also used to answer questions pertaining to issues of public health. Epidemiologist John Snow’s 1854 London cholera map was created to record instances of cholera in a London neighborhood, pinpointing the cause of the outbreak to a single well. This knowledge gained from patterns in lists of names, numbers, and locations was then used to persuade London’s populace to install sewer systems to alleviate the proliferation and spread of disease. The human brain is particularly adept at recognizing patterns, and a good data visualization, like Snow’s, optimizes displays of these patterns through effective use of Gestalt theory, design principles, and color. (Or lack of it, as this case may be.)

早期的可视化数据也被用来回答有关公共健康的问题。流行病学家约翰·斯诺的1854年伦敦霍乱地图的建立是为了记录霍乱的实例在伦敦附近，查明暴发的原因。从图案的名称，数量和位置列表获得这方面的知识，然后用来说服伦敦的民众安装排水系统，以纾缓扩散和疾病的传播。人类的大脑是特别擅长识别模式，以及良好的数据可视化，喜欢雪的，通过有效利用格式塔理论，设计原理和色彩优化这些模式的显示。 （或缺乏它，因为这种情况而定。）

Snow’s visualization, with its absence of color, optimizes Gestalt’s theories of visual perception, most notably “Proximity” and “Figure and Ground.” The small black dots, each one representing a single case of cholera are small black figures standing out in contrast against the ground: in this graphic, the lines and white space representing streets. The proximity of these dots around the affected well are what enabled Snow to determine the exact source of the outbreak. Today, even with our advanced computing systems and sophisticated tools for creating data visualizations, there is little you could do to improve the effectiveness of this chart. It is simple, beautiful, and true: a data visualization that saved lives.

斯诺的可视化，以其无颜色，视觉感知优化的格式塔理论，最明显的是“接近”和“图和地。”小的黑点，每一个代表霍乱个案都是黑色的小数字一一对比对地面：在这个图形，线条和空白代表街道。周围受影响的还有这些点附近是什么使雪来确定爆发的确切来源。今天，即使我们先进的计算机系统和复杂的工具，用于创建数据的可视化，有一点你可以做些什么来改善这个图表的效果。它是简单的，美丽的，真实的：拯救了生活中的数据可视化。

Florence Nightingale, famous more for her nursing skills than her analytic prowess, was nonetheless also a master data scientist and storyteller. Through data presented via her signature Coxcomb diagram (also known as polar or rose charts), she convinced the British army to invest in sanitation measures after illustrating that the majority of deaths in the Crimean War were the result of preventable diseases caused by the horrible sanitary conditions in hospitals. “Why are we sick?” she asked, then answering the question herself by giving visual form to data.

佛罗伦萨·南丁格尔，她的护理技能比她分析实力更著名，是无论如何也是数据科学家和讲故事的人。经由她的签名花花公子图给出的数据（也称为极性或玫瑰图），她说服了英国军队中说明，大部分在克里米亚战争中死亡的人所造成的可怕的卫生预防疾病的结果后，卫生措施，投资条件的医院。“我们为什么生病了吗？”她问，那么回答这个问题由自己来提供数据可视化的形式。

Looking at this graph, it is readily apparent that preventable diseases outnumbered all other causes of death. The area in blue represents deaths by preventable diseases, measured from the center, with red representing deaths caused by injuries and black indicating all other causes. Design principles at play here include the addition of color theory to take advantage of more Gestalt principles: “Similarity” and “Continuity”. Color makes it easy for us to tell which segments belong to which category. It also helps to draw the eye in a continuous path around the graphic, making it easier to read.

在看这个曲线图中，它是显而易见的是可预防的疾病人数超过死亡的所有其他原因。蓝色区域代表由可预防的疾病死亡，从中心测定，所造成的伤害和黑色显示所有其他的原因红色代表死亡。在图中的设计原则包括除了色彩理论采取更多格式塔原则的优势：“相似性”和“连续性”。颜色很容易让我们分辨出哪段属于哪个类别。这也有助于吸引眼球，在图形周围的连续路径，使其更易于阅读。

There is debate over the quality of this chart. Some claim it one of the best, most memorable visualizations ever created, not solely because of its visual communication strength, but in spite of it. It is remembered because of the change it inspired. Others deride it, claiming it’s just a glorified pie chart, suffering from the same misrepresentation of the information by distorting the data: in terms of visual perception, humans have a hard time accurately judging measures represented by differences in area. Despite their ubiquity, pie charts, for this very reason, are an incredibly poor way to visualize data. A simple stacked bar chart with reference lines, while not as beautiful or visually intriguing, would have communicated more effectively and on a quicker read.

有人争论这张图的质量。有些人声称它曾经尽管最好的，最令人难忘的视觉效果之一的视觉传播力量。因为它激发了变化才想起。别人嘲笑它，声称它只是一个华而不实的饼图，通过扭曲的数据信息的虚假陈述一样痛苦：就可视化观感而言，人类也很难准确地在不同地区代表判断的措施。尽管饼图他们无处不在，因为这个原因，有一个令人难以置信的差的方式来可视化数据。一个简单的堆积条形图参考行，而不是漂亮的或视觉上吸引人的，更快的读取，会更有效地沟通。

We still ask the same kinds of questions that Snow and Nightingale posed, and as curious humans, probably always will. But the major differences today are that we have the means with which to gather much larger, richer datasets. And we also have the tools with which to automate visualizing our data.

我们仍然要求同种的雪和夜莺冒充好奇的人的问题，而且很可能永远会。但是，今天的主要区别是，我们与这些聚集更大，更丰富的数据集的手段。我们也有与该工具自动可视化数据。

This isn’t to say that small datasets, like the ones Nightingale and Snow observed, are any less valuable or interesting. Thanks to data gathering technology and advances in wearable computing and the Internet of Things, to the contrary. My friend Jen Lowe, a data scientist and artist working in New York, recently published her heartbeat on the internet. As a flat, static visualization, it would be beautiful but not especially informative. But by adding interactivity and live data feeds of her pulse via wearable sensors and API calls, her heartbeat is a living, beating, digital thing, viewable by anyone, anywhere, at any time. What you have is insight into another human being like you’ve never seen before.

这是不是说小的数据集，像那些夜莺与斯诺观察，任何没有价值的或有趣的。由于数据采集技术和Ad-万斯夫妇在可穿戴计算和物联网，与此相反。我的朋友仁·罗威，数据科学家和艺术家在纽约工作，近日把她的心跳发布到互联网上。作为一个单位，静态可视化，这将是美丽的，但不是特别丰富。但通过增加交互性和实时数据源，通过可穿戴传感器和API调用她的脉搏，她的心跳是有生命的，也好，数字的东西，可见任何人，任何地方，任何时间。你有什么洞察另一个人，就像你从未见过的。

Unique insight is the essence of data, both big and small, and the result of the tools that allow us to access, probe, poke, prod, dissect, visualize, and hopefully, make sense of it. Tools which, through the democratization of data visualization, allow us to change our lens on the world, creating pictures of humanity from different perspectives, bringing into focus stories about humanity and the world that were previously invisible, allowing us insight into ourselves like we’ve never seen before.

独特见解是数据，既大又小，那让我们来访问，探头，戳，督促，对开，可视化，并希望，理解它的工具，其结果的精髓。工具，通过数据可视化的民主化，使我们能够改变我们的镜头对世界，创造人类从不同角度的照片，将成为左右人类和世界，以前看不见的传奇故事，让我们见识到自己，就像我们“从没见过。

Callie Neylan
Senior Designer, Microsoft

凯丽·妮蓝
